# PaisaWatch Podcast: Full Two-Host Script (English)

Two hosts. RIYA is warm and curious, the listener's stand-in. ARJUN explains, a little
wry. Target run time about 12 to 14 minutes at a natural pace. Keep it conversational.
Read the reactions ("hmm", "wait, really") as real, not scripted. No em dashes.

Voices suggestion: RIYA female, ARJUN male, so the two are easy to tell apart.

---

RIYA: Okay Arjun, quick question, and be honest. Can you name your own MP?

ARJUN: My MP. Right now. Full name.

RIYA: Full name.

ARJUN: See, this is a trap, because I know the party, I know the face, and the name is
just sitting on the tip of my tongue and refusing to come.

RIYA: That is exactly the point. Most of us cannot. And here is the follow-up that stings
a little more. Do you know where even one rupee of your tax actually went last year?

ARJUN: No. Genuinely, no. It leaves my salary before I even see it, and then it just,
disappears into the idea of "the government."

RIYA: So today we are talking about a free tool that tries to fix exactly that. It is
called PaisaWatch. And the whole thing rests on one simple idea. There are two numbers in
your financial life that nobody ever shows you.

ARJUN: Two hidden numbers. Go on.

RIYA: The first one is personal. Your raise.

ARJUN: My favourite email of the year.

RIYA: Right. It feels like progress. But a raise is what they call a nominal number. And
inflation is basically a tax that nobody sends you a bill for. So say your salary went up
9 percent, and prices that year went up 6 percent. How much richer are you, actually?

ARJUN: I want to say 9 percent, but the way you are asking makes me think the answer is
sad.

RIYA: It is about 3 percent. And that is before income tax takes its cut of the raise
itself. Some years, when inflation runs hot, the raise and the price rise basically cancel
out, and you have worked all year just to stand in the same place.

ARJUN: And nobody tells you that, because the 9 percent is the number that feels good.

RIYA: The 9 percent feels good. The 3 percent is the honest one. And most people never
run it.

ARJUN: Okay, that is the personal number. What is the second one?

RIYA: The second one is public. Think about the single biggest thing you will ever buy in
your life. Not a house. Not a car.

ARJUN: Bigger than a house.

RIYA: Over a whole working life, the tax you pay is bigger than a house. It leaves every
month, before your salary even lands. And it becomes roads, schools, hospitals, defence.
It is the most expensive thing you will ever buy, you pay for it in installments for forty
years, and you have never once been handed a receipt.

ARJUN: That is the part that gets me. I know exactly what my phone bill bought me. I have
no idea what my tax bought.

RIYA: And that gap, that is the whole reason this tool exists. PaisaWatch is basically the
receipt.

ARJUN: So what do you actually see when you open it?

RIYA: Three things. One, a map of public money. It tracks public works spending across
India, state by state, sector by sector, from official government data.

ARJUN: Give me a real number. Make it concrete.

RIYA: Sure. Right now the tool tracks about 10,839 crore rupees of this public works
spending. And you can see where it went. Roads, rail and bridges, about 4,500 crore.
Public facilities, about 2,600. Education, about 1,500. Drinking water, sanitation and
health, smaller slices.

ARJUN: So I can literally see, in my state, this much went to roads, this much to water.

RIYA: Exactly. Thing two is the one that makes people sit up. You can look up your own
representative. Type your constituency, and you see what your MP or MLA declared about
themselves. Their assets, their pending court cases, their education, and for MPs, their
attendance in Parliament.

ARJUN: Wait. Declared by them. This is not gossip, this is their own filing.

RIYA: Their own affidavit. The tool currently covers 485 Lok Sabha MPs, and about 3,725
MLAs across 30 assemblies. And it will tell you, for example, how many of them declared
assets over a crore, or how many declared a pending criminal case.

ARJUN: Okay but hold on, and I think this matters a lot. A pending case is not the same as
being guilty.

RIYA: Thank you for saying that, because it is the most important rule in the whole thing.
Pending is not proven. A pending case is a declared, unresolved legal matter. It is not a
conviction. And the tool does not ask you to take its word for it. Every single figure
links back to the original record, so you can open it and judge for yourself.

ARJUN: So the tool is not pointing a finger. It is handing you the file.

RIYA: It is handing you the file. And that is a deliberate choice, because the moment this
becomes "this party bad, that party good", it dies. So the villain here is never a party.
The villain is opacity. The dark you were kept in.

ARJUN: I actually really like that framing, because it means someone on either side can
share it, and neither can call it biased.

RIYA: That is the entire strategy in one sentence. Stay factual, link the source, and let
people decide.

ARJUN: You said three things. Map, lookup, and the third?

RIYA: The third is the everyday-money part. Calculators. There is an income tax estimator,
an EMI calculator, an investment calculator, and the one we started with, the salary
versus inflation check. You put in what you earned a few years ago and what you earn now,
and in about ten seconds it tells you the real change.

ARJUN: The honest 3 percent instead of the flattering 9.

RIYA: The honest number. No login, no ads, completely free.

ARJUN: Let me push on the trust side once more, because people are rightly sceptical of
anything that looks like an attack site. What stops this from just being outrage bait?

RIYA: A few rules, and they are baked in. One, declared is not audited. Affidavit numbers
are self-declared by the candidate, and the tool says so. Two, absence of data is data
too. When there is no verifiable public record for someone, it does not invent one, it
shows a coverage count and says "we could not find this". So if it says 485 of 543, that
just means the rest have no published affidavit. Not that they are clean, not that they
are guilty.

ARJUN: And three?

RIYA: Three, size is not a scandal. A big state spends big sums. A large number is not
wrongdoing by itself. The real question is what the money bought, and whether it was
tendered fairly.

ARJUN: That is a very grown-up way to build something like this. So why does it matter?
Beyond, you know, being interesting.

RIYA: Because when records are hard to find, the incentive to cut corners quietly grows.
And the cost of checking falls on ordinary people who have neither the time nor the tools.
A democracy that runs on rumour instead of records is a fragile thing. What we actually
need is boring and powerful. One official, machine readable, regularly updated public
register. Who represents you, what they declared, and how public funds were used. Free,
for anyone, forever.

ARJUN: And this is a citizen built step toward that.

RIYA: Made from records that already existed, but were never made easy to read.

ARJUN: Okay. If I am listening to this on my commute, what do I do the second I get off?

RIYA: Two minutes. Open paisawatch.live. Look up your own constituency, read what your MP
or MLA declared, and open the source. Then go to the calculators and check whether your
last raise actually beat inflation.

ARJUN: And then?

RIYA: And then you just, know. That is it. The country does not change because you looked.
But you do. And a country full of people who have seen the honest number is a much harder
country to keep in the dark.

ARJUN: PaisaWatch. Free, non-partisan, source-linked. Go look up your own seat.

RIYA: paisawatch.live. Check your money. Then check theirs.

ARJUN: See you next time.
